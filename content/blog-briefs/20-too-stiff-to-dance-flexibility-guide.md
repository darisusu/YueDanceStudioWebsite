## Outline
- **Intro hook** — the toes-out-of-reach moment; promise a safe, realistic starting framework, not a contortionist's regimen.
- **H2 — The myth: "I have to be flexible before I can start."** Flexibility is an *outcome* of practice, not a prerequisite. Studios teach adult beginners at exactly this starting point every term; stiffness is the norm. You get flexible *by* dancing (with structured stretching).
- **H2 — How flexibility actually improves (general, non-clinical).** Two plain ideas: (a) the nervous system gradually *allows* more range as a movement feels safe and familiar; (b) with consistent, gentle loading, tissues adapt over time. **Consistency beats intensity.** No fabricated numbers or studies — mechanism in general terms only.
- **H2 — Dynamic vs static stretching: when to use each.** H3 Before class: dynamic (leg swings, ankle circles, arm/torso rotations) to warm and prime; long static holds on a cold body aren't the goal. H3 After class / dedicated session: static & longer holds, ease in, breathe — better for developing range once warm. Takeaway line: **dynamic to prepare, static to develop.**
- **H2 — The areas dancers care about (and why).** Brief, encouraging tour, described generally (not prescribed exercises): Hips (turnout, extensions, mobility); Hamstrings (the classic "can't reach my toes"; lines and lifts); Ankles & feet (pointing, balance, articulation — often overlooked); Spine/back (curves, épaulement, expressive upper body — treat gently); Shoulders (port de bras, openness, carriage). Each "trainable over time."
- **H2 — A simple, realistic weekly approach.** Little and often beats one heroic session. Always warm up first; work into ranges gradually; never bounce or force. Pair stretching with attending class. Rest/recovery are part of progress.
- **H2 — A sample gentle routine (in principle, not rep-by-rep).** Describe shape and intent: a short dynamic warm-up; a few easy, well-supported static stretches for key areas afterward; comfortable holds with steady breathing; stop well short of pain. A template to adapt; an instructor tailors it in class. Do **not** prescribe exact hold times/angles or aggressive techniques as medical fact.
- **H2 — Safety & red flags (dedicated section).** Warm up before stretching; move gradually; mild stretch discomfort is normal — sharp, shooting, or joint pain is not. Never force, bounce, or "push through" pain. Red flags to stop and seek help: sharp/sudden pain, joint pain (not muscle belly), numbness/tingling, swelling, lingering pain. **Explicit disclaimer:** general educational guidance, not medical advice; consult a doctor or physiotherapist before starting if you have pain, an injury, or a medical condition.
- **H2 — Realistic timelines.** Honest: meaningful change is typically weeks to months, and varies by individual/starting point/consistency. No "splits in 30 days." Celebrate small wins (fingertips past mid-shin).
- **Closing** — you're not too stiff; you're at the beginning, which is a fine place to be. CTA to a trial.
